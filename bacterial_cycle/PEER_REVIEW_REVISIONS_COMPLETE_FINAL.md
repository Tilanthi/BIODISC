#### 2. AsI Formulation: Dimensional Normalization NOW FULLY SPECIFIED ✅
**Problem**: σM and σP were not specified over what population they're computed; no guidance for σP ≈ 0 cases.

**Solution**: Added comprehensive specification of dimensional normalization.

**Change Made** (Section 7.1):
```
ADDED: "Specification of σM and σP"

**Population definition**:
- Cell-to-cell variation: σ computed from n ≥ 100 individual cells under identical baseline conditions
- Temporal variation: Alternatively, σ computed from time-series measurements of single cells
- Recommended: Use cell-to-cell variation as primary metric

**Statistical requirements**:
- Minimum sample size: n ≥ 50 for preliminary; n ≥ 100 for publication-quality
- Measurement conditions: All baseline measurements under identical growth conditions
- Exclusion criteria: Remove outliers > 3 MAD from median

**Handling edge cases**:
- σP ≈ 0: Report AsI as "AsI >> 1" with explicit note of σP value
- σM ≈ 0: Similar reporting
- Both ≈ 0: AsI cannot be reliably computed from these data
```

**Impact**: Makes AsI calculation reproducible with clear population definitions and edge case handling.

**Solution**: Added explicit justification for inclusion in cell cycle framework.

**Change Made** (Section 2.3.1):

```
ADDED: "Why Include MreB in a Cell Cycle Framework?"

MreB is primarily a cell shape maintenance system rather than a core cell cycle
regulator like FtsZ, DnaA, or SMC complexes. However, we include MreB in this
discussion because:

1. Shape-coupling to division: Cell elongation (MreB-driven) must be coordinated

2. Size checkpoint: Cell size at division depends on integrated outcome of both
   elongation and division processes
3. Physical-molecular interface: Like other systems discussed, MreB illustrates
   how molecular systems interface with physical constraints (geometry, curvature)

**Important caveat**: MreB is not strictly a cell cycle regulator in the same sense
as FtsZ or DnaA. Its inclusion is justified by its role in coordinating cell
growth with division, creating the physical context in which cell cycle decisions
are made.
```

**Impact**: Explicit justification resolves category confusion.

#### 17. Novelty Comparison to Kirschner/Koch: NOW ADDED ✅
**Problem**: Not clearly distinguished from "molecular regulation constrains physical processes" (Kirschner/Mitchison) and Koch's surface stress theory.

**Solution**: Added explicit comparison section.

**Change Made** (Section 8.1):
```
ADDED: "Relation to Previous Work on Physical-Molecular Integration"

**Kirschner, Mitchison, and cytoskeletal dynamics (1980s-1990s)**: Work on
eukaryotic cytoskeletal dynamics emphasized that "molecular regulation constrains
physical processes" — molecular systems determine when and where cytoskeletal
structures assemble, overriding physical self-organization tendencies. Our
bacterial framework is conceptually similar but makes the additional claim of
**asymmetric information flow**: molecular systems read physical states, but
physical states do not read or respond to molecular states. The asymmetry claim
is what requires experimental validation through AsI measurements.

**Koch's surface stress theory (1983)**: Koch proposed that cell wall growth
patterns emerge from physical stress distribution, with molecular systems following
rather than directing physical forces. Our framework explicitly **challenges**
this view for bacterial cell cycle regulation: we argue that molecular systems
actively override physical tendencies when they would produce incorrect outcomes,
not merely follow them.

**What makes our contribution distinct**: (1) We propose a **quantitative metric**
(AsI) to test information flow directionality, (2) We emphasize **asymmetry** rather
than bidirectional coupling, and (3) We provide an **experimental roadmap** with
explicit falsification criteria.
```

**Impact**: Clear differentiation from previous work; novelty now properly framed.

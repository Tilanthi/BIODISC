## The Core Problems Identified by the Reviewer

### Problem 1: Co-Evolution vs. Physical-First Presented as "Equally Consistent"

**Reviewer's point**:
> "The co-evolution scenario (Scenario B) is presented as 'equally consistent with current data' as the physical-first scenario, without engaging with the most relevant evidence that would distinguish them."

**The problem**: The manuscript framed the evolutionary question as choosing between two simplistic scenarios:
- **Scenario A (Physical-First)**: Early protocells relied on physical constraints → later evolved FtsZ-based division → then evolved molecular regulation
- **Scenario B (Co-Evolution)**: Physical constraints and molecular regulation co-evolved from early stages

Both presented as "equally consistent with current data," but this framing **ignores the most relevant evidence** that would distinguish between them: the diversity of ancestral division mechanisms.

### Problem 2: Synthetic Cell Biology Results Misinterpreted

**Reviewer's point**:
> "Synthetic reconstitution experiments establish the current minimum requirements for a functional division system, not its evolutionary minimum. A key distinction that the manuscript does not make."

**The problem**: The manuscript cited García et al., 2021 and Pineda et al., 2022 as evidence favoring co-evolution, stating: "Synthetic cell biology suggesting that even minimal functional cell cycles require substantial molecular infrastructure (Garcia et al., 2021; Pineda et al., 2022) may favor co-evolution scenarios."

**Critical distinction missed**:
- **Current minimum**: What components are required to build a functional division system **today**
- **Evolutionary minimum**: What components early protocells actually used **billions of years ago**

Just because modern systems require FtsZ, FtsA, and membrane-targeting sequences does **NOT** imply that early evolution used these same components. Evolution often finds multiple solutions to the same functional problem.

### Problem 3: FtsZ Is Likely Not the Ancestral Mechanism

**Reviewer's point**:
> "FtsZ-based division is itself likely not the ancestral mechanism. Several lines of evidence (ESCRT-III-related CdvB proteins in archaea, the FtsZ-independent division of Chlamydiae, diverse division mechanisms in Planctomycetes) suggest that the last common ancestor of extant bacterial division systems may have used very different machinery."

**The problem**: Both evolutionary scenarios (Physical-First and Co-Evolution) assume FtsZ is central:
- **Physical-First**: Assumes physical scission → later evolved **FtsZ-based** division → then evolved regulation
- **Co-Evolution**: Assumes physical constraints and **FtsZ-based** molecular regulation co-evolved

But FtsZ itself may be a **derived innovation** that replaced earlier systems! The diversity of division mechanisms suggests the last common ancestor of bacteria may have used a completely different (and now lost) mechanism.

## Solution Implemented: Comprehensive Evolutionary Section Revision

### Part 1: Added Critical Distinction (Current Minimum vs. Evolutionary Minimum)

**BEFORE** (Misleading):
> "Synthetic cell biology suggests that even minimal functional cell cycles require substantial molecular infrastructure (Garcia et al., 2021; Pineda et al., 2022), which may favor co-evolution scenarios"

**AFTER** (Accurate):
> **Critical Distinction: Current Minimum vs. Evolutionary Minimum**
>
> **A fundamental methodological limitation**: Synthetic reconstitution experiments (Garcia et al., 2021; Pineda et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2021) establish the **current minimum requirements** for functional cell division systems. These experiments tell us what molecular components are required to achieve division **in modern reconstructed systems**. They do **NOT** establish the **evolutionary minimum**—the minimal machinery that early protocells or LUCA actually used.
>
> **Why this distinction matters**:
> - **Current minimum**: What components are required to build a functional division system **today**, given the evolutionary constraints and historical contingency that have shaped modern systems
> - **Evolutionary minimum**: What components early protocells actually used **billions of years ago**, which may have been radically different from modern systems
>
> Just because modern minimal systems require certain components (e.g., FtsZ, FtsA, membrane-targeting sequences) does **NOT** imply that early evolutionary systems required these same components. Evolution often finds multiple solutions to the same functional problem.

### Part 2: Engaged with Ancestral Division Mechanism Literature

**BEFORE** (Incomplete):
> "Both physical-first and co-evolution scenarios are consistent with current data."

**AFTER** (Comprehensive):
> **Ancestral Division Mechanisms: Beyond FtsZ**
>
> **A critical oversight in previous versions of this manuscript**: We presented the evolutionary question as choosing between "physical-first" and "co-evolution" scenarios, but we **failed to engage with the most relevant evidence** that would distinguish between these scenarios: the **diversity of ancestral division mechanisms**.
>
> **Multiple lines of evidence suggest that FtsZ-based division is NOT the ancestral mechanism**:
>
> **1. ESCRT-III Systems in Archaea**:
> - Archaea use ESCRT-III-like systems for cell division, including CdvB proteins that are homologous to eukaryotic ESCRT-III machinery (Lindås et al., 2008; Samson et al., 2008; Baum et al., 2022)
> - The ESCRT-III system is fundamentally different from FtsZ-based division: it uses membrane-sculpting filaments that constrict from the **inside** of the membrane, rather than FtsZ's Z-ring that constricts from the **outside**
> - Phylogenetic analyses suggest that ESCRT-III systems are ancient and may predate FtsZ in the tree of life (Dawson et al., 2023)
> - **Implication**: The last common ancestor of archaea and bacteria (or even earlier protocells) may have used ESCRT-like or other primitive division machinery, not FtsZ
>
> **2. FtsZ-Independent Division in Chlamydiae**:
> - Members of the Chlamydiae phylum (e.g., *Chlamydia trachomatis*, *Candidatus Protochlamydia amoebophila*) **lack FtsZ entirely** and instead use MreB and other proteins for division (Stephens et al., 1998; Pillai et al., 2023; Abdelrahman et al., 2023)
> - Chlamydiae divide by an unusual mechanism involving MreB-based septum formation and recruitment of division proteins **without** a canonical FtsZ Z-ring
> - The Chlamydiae lineage is ancient, branching off early in bacterial evolution
> - **Implication**: FtsZ is **not universal** even among bacteria, and alternative division mechanisms can evolve and persist
>
> **3. Diverse Division Mechanisms in Planctomycetes and Other PVC Superphylum Members**:
> - Planctomycetes often divide by **budding** rather than binary fission, and many lack FtsZ or have highly divergent FtsZ homologs (Jogler et al., 2012; Rivas-Marín et al., 2016; Wiegmann et al., 2020)
> - Some Planctomycetes use novel division proteins (e.g., PodJ, RodA) that are unrelated to the classical divisome
> - Members of the PVC superphylum (Planctomycetes, Verrucomicrobia, Chlamydiae) show remarkable diversity in division mechanisms
> - **Implication**: Multiple, mechanistically distinct division strategies have evolved, suggesting that the last common ancestor of bacteria may have used a different, more primitive system

### Part 3: Revised Evolutionary Scenarios

**BEFORE** (Simplistic two scenarios):
> **Scenario A: Physical-First, Molecular-Layers-Added**
> - Early protocells relied heavily on physical constraints for division
> - Molecular regulation evolved later as an overlay on physical processes
>
> **Scenario B: Co-Evolution from Early Stages**
> - Physical constraints and molecular regulation co-evolved from the earliest stages of cellular life
> - Even minimal protocells may have required significant molecular infrastructure to achieve functional division

**AFTER** (Nuanced scenarios acknowledging diversity):
> **Revised Evolutionary Scenarios (All Speculative, None Strongly Supported)**:
>
> Given the diversity of division mechanisms, simplistic scenarios like "physical-first vs. co-evolution" are inadequate. More plausible (but still speculative) scenarios include:
>
> **Scenario A: Physical-First, with Lost Ancestral Systems**
> - Early protocells divided by physical scission or primitive mechanisms (perhaps ESCRT-like or other unknown systems)
> - Multiple independent division systems evolved later (FtsZ-based, MreB-based, budding mechanisms)
> - Different lineages retained or lost different systems
> - **Problem**: Highly speculative; no direct evidence
>
> **Scenario B: Early Molecular Diversity with Convergence**
> - Early protocells had diverse, competing molecular division mechanisms
> - Modern diversity reflects this early experimentation, not linear progression
> - FtsZ, ESCRT-III, MreB-based systems all have ancient origins
> - **Problem**: Cannot be tested with current data
>
> **Scenario C: Unknown Ancestral Mechanism**
> - The last common ancestor used a division mechanism that has no modern analogue
> - All modern systems (FtsZ, ESCRT-III, MreB-based) are derived innovations that replaced the ancestral system
> - The ancestral mechanism is lost to deep time
> - **Problem**: Fundamentally untestable
>
> **Honest assessment**: ALL scenarios are speculative. The diversity of division mechanisms across extant organisms suggests that early cell cycle evolution was **more complex and reticulate** than simple linear models allow. Current evidence **CANNOT** distinguish between these scenarios.

- Physical-only division → FtsZ-based division → Regulated FtsZ-based division

**AFTER** (Recognition of ancestral diversity):

## The Core Problem Identified by the Reviewer

### Issue 1: Specific Evolutionary Narrative Not Distinguished from Alternative

**Problematic statements throughout the manuscript**:

1. Line 553 (Section 5.1): "The hierarchical framework has implications for origins of life research—it suggests that early protocells may have relied more heavily on physical constraints, with molecular regulation evolving later."

2. Line 1804: "**Evolutionary implications**: Suggests molecular regulation evolved as layers added to physical foundations, not as parallel systems from the beginning"

3. Line 1813: "**Evolution adds hierarchical layers** (not symmetric coupling): Evolution proceeds by adding molecular override layers on top of physical foundations, not by creating balanced bidirectional coupling."

**The problem**: These statements present a specific evolutionary trajectory (physical-first, molecular layers added) as plausible or implied by the framework, without adequately acknowledging that the co-evolution alternative (molecular and physical systems developed together from early stages) is equally consistent with limited data.

### Issue 2: Synthetic Cell Biology Results Not Engaged

**Reviewer's point**: "The paper does not engage adequately with synthetic cell biology results suggesting that even minimal functional cell cycles may require significant molecular infrastructure from the outset."

**Key synthetic cell biology findings that should be engaged**:
- Bottom-up approaches show that controlled, reproducible division requires sophisticated molecular organization
- Reconstituted division systems need coordinated activity of multiple components
- Even "minimal" cells (syn3.0) require ~19 cell cycle genes for any functional division

**The implication**: These findings may favor co-evolution scenarios over strict physical-first models.

## Solution Implemented: Comprehensive Revision of Evolutionary Discussions

### Part 1: Section 5.1 Completely Rewritten

**BEFORE** (Problematic):
> "The hierarchical framework has implications for origins of life research—it suggests that early protocells may have relied more heavily on physical constraints, with molecular regulation evolving later. However, this evolutionary trajectory remains speculative..."

**AFTER** (Corrected):

> **Alternative Evolutionary Trajectories (Both Speculative):**
>
> The evolutionary relationship between physical constraints and molecular regulation in early cell cycles is fundamentally uncertain. Multiple scenarios are consistent with current data:
>
> **Scenario A: Physical-First, Molecular-Layers-Added (Traditional Hierarchical View)**
> - Early protocells relied heavily on physical constraints for division
> - Molecular regulation evolved later as an overlay on physical processes
> - **Evidence cited**: JCVI-syn3.0 minimal cells divide with high variability
> - **Limitations**: syn3.0 interpretation confounded by abnormal morphology, slow growth, pleiotropic defects
>
> **Scenario B: Co-Evolution from Early Stages (Alternative View)**
> - Physical constraints and molecular regulation co-evolved from the earliest stages
> - Even minimal protocells may have required significant molecular infrastructure
> - **Evidence from synthetic cell biology**: Bottom-up approaches demonstrate that achieving controlled, reproducible division requires sophisticated molecular organization
> - **Key insight**: The "minimal" requirements for functional cell cycles may be much higher than suggested by simple physical-first scenarios
>
> **Critical Caveat on Evolutionary Claims**:
>
> The manuscript has sometimes implied a "physical-first, molecular-layers-added" evolutionary narrative. **This narrative is speculative and not strongly supported by available evidence.** A more honest statement is:
>
> > "The evolutionary relationship between physical constraints and molecular regulation in early cell cycles is UNKNOWN. Both physical-first and co-evolution scenarios are consistent with current data. Synthetic cell biology suggests that even minimal functional cell cycles require substantial molecular infrastructure, which may favor co-evolution scenarios, but this interpretation remains provisional."

### Part 2: Synthetic Cell Biology Results Integrated

**New "Synthetic Cell Biology Results" subsection added**:

> **Synthetic Cell Biology Results Relevant to Evolutionary Claims:**
>
> Recent work in synthetic cell biology and bottom-up origins research provides critical insights that challenge simple physical-first narratives:
>
> **Minimal Cell Requirements**:
> - **JCVI-syn3.0** (Hutchison et al., 2016; Pelletier et al., 2021): Despite having only 473 genes, syn3.0 requires ~19 cell cycle genes (CCGC ≈ 19) for division. Even this "minimal" system has substantial molecular regulatory complexity.
> - **Key observation**: syn3.0 shows high division variability (CV ≈ 0.35-0.45) compared to wild-type E. coli (CV ≈ 0.10-0.15). However, this does NOT necessarily mean "physical defaults are exposed"—alternative explanations include pleiotropic defects, geometric constraints, and growth rate effects.
>
> **Bottom-Up Synthetic Cell Approaches**:
> - **Liposome division systems** (Hanczyc et al., 2003; Zhu et al., 2021): Physical scission of lipid vesicles can occur without molecular machinery, but achieving reproducible division timing and placement requires sophisticated molecular control systems
> - **Gene-reconstituted systems** (Garcia et al., 2021; Pineda et al., 2022): Reconstituting cell division in vitro requires coordinated activity of multiple molecular components (FtsZ, FtsA, ZipA, membrane-targeting sequences)
> - **Key insight**: While physical processes CAN drive division without molecular regulation, achieving PRECISE and REPRODUCIBLE division appears to require molecular control even in minimal systems

### Part 3: Section 8 Evolutionary Implications Updated

**BEFORE** (Line 1804):
> "3. **Evolutionary implications**: Suggests molecular regulation evolved as layers added to physical foundations, not as parallel systems from the beginning"

**AFTER** (Corrected):
> "3. **Evolutionary implications**: The hierarchical framework is compatible with multiple evolutionary scenarios. One possibility is that molecular regulation evolved as layers added to physical foundations. However, **co-evolution scenarios**—where physical and molecular systems developed together from early stages—are equally consistent with current data. Synthetic cell biology results showing that even minimal functional cell cycles require substantial molecular infrastructure (Garcia et al., 2021; Pineda et al., 2022) may favor co-evolution scenarios, but this interpretation remains provisional. We do NOT claim strong support for any specific evolutionary trajectory."

### Part 4: Section 10.4 Origins of Life Research Completely Revised

**BEFORE** (Lines 2676-2678):
> "- Physical constraints likely provided some organizational logic in early protocells **[speculative]**
> - **However**: Early protocells likely had **imprecise, inefficient** cell cycles with substantial variability **[speculative—direct evidence lacking]**
> - Evolution of molecular regulation was essential for achieving the precision observed in modern bacteria **[well-established]**"

**AFTER** (Corrected):

> **Two plausible evolutionary scenarios** (both speculative, neither strongly supported):
>
> **Scenario A - Physical-First**:
> - Physical constraints provided some organizational logic in early protocells **[speculative]**
> - Early protocells had imprecise, inefficient cell cycles with substantial variability **[speculative—direct evidence lacking]**
> - Molecular regulation evolved later as layers added to physical processes **[speculative]**
>
> **Scenario B - Co-Evolution** (alternative compatible with synthetic cell biology data):
> - Physical constraints and molecular regulation co-evolved from early stages
> - Even early protocells likely required substantial molecular infrastructure for functional division
> - **Evidence from synthetic cell biology**: Minimal functional division systems require sophisticated molecular organization (Garcia et al., 2021; Pineda et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2021)
> - **Implication**: Co-evolution scenario may be more plausible than strict physical-first models
>
> **Well-established**: Evolution of molecular regulation was essential for achieving the precision observed in modern bacteria

**BEFORE** (Line 2694):
> "- How does evolution add molecular layers while maintaining integration with physical constraints?"

**AFTER** (Corrected):
> "- **Evolutionary question**: Did evolution add molecular layers to pre-existing physical foundations, OR did physical and molecular systems co-evolve from early stages? Current evidence cannot distinguish between these scenarios."

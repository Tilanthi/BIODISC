**Problematic statement** (Section 10.5, line 2759):

**The problem**:
1. **Caricatured simplification**: This presents Noble's position as a simple hierarchical control model
2. **Misses the subtlety**: Noble's actual argument is about the **relational nature** of causation across levels
3. **Misrepresents "no privileged level"**: Noble's central thesis is that no single level of causation should be privileged
4. **Overstates "downward causation"**: Noble uses this concept to critique reductionism, not to propose top-down control

**Reviewer's point**: "Noble does not claim that 'higher levels exert downward causal influence on lower levels' in a simple hierarchical sense; his argument is subtler, concerning the relational nature of causation across levels. ## Noble (2012) Actual Position

**Paper**: Noble, D. (2012). "A theory of biological relativity: No privileged level of causation." *Interface Focus* 2: 55-64.

**Key points of Noble's argument**:
1. **Against privileging any single level**: No level (genes, cells, tissues, organisms) should be given causal priority
2. **Relational nature of causation**: Biological causation operates through a web of reciprocal constraints across levels
3. **Critique of reductionism**: Reducing all causation to the lowest level (genes/molecules) fails to capture biological reality
4. **Downward causation as interconnectedness**: Higher-level properties emerge from and constrain lower-level processes, but this is not simple top-down control
5. **Biological relativity**: Just as physical relativity shows there's no privileged reference frame, biological causation has no privileged level

**What Noble does NOT claim**:
- Simple hierarchical control from higher to lower levels
- That "higher levels exert downward causal influence" in the sense of top-down commands
- A unidirectional causal flow from organisms to molecules

## Solution Implemented: Accurate Representation of Noble's Position

### Complete Revision of Noble (2012) Discussion

**BEFORE** (Caricatured):
> "Noble argues that biological causation operates across multiple levels, with higher levels (organisms, tissues) exerting downward causal influence on lower levels (genes, molecules). Our hierarchical framework extends this to the physical-molecular interface: molecular systems (higher organizational level) can detect and override physical constraints (lower level), but the converse does not occur."

**AFTER** (Accurate):
> "Noble argues against privileging any single level of causation, emphasizing instead that biological causation is fundamentally relational across levels. His critique of reductionism centers on the insight that higher-level organizational properties emerge from and constrain lower-level processes, but this does not imply simple hierarchical control. Rather, causation operates through a web of reciprocal constraints across levels. Our hierarchical framework makes a different claim: we argue that at the physical-molecular interface, the relationship is specifically **asymmetric** under functional conditions. Molecular systems can detect and respond to physical constraints, but physical states do not exhibit compensatory responses to maintain molecular functions. Our framework thus departs from Noble's emphasis on relational reciprocity by proposing context-dependent asymmetry—molecular systems read physical states, but physical states do not 'read' or respond to molecular states in a functionally directed way."
